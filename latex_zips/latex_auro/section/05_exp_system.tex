As $\tau$ increases, the average number of queries generally increases in both domains. In waste sorting, increasing $\tau$ from $0.6$ to $0.7$ changes the success rate from $58.0\%$ to $92.5\%$, the average number of queries from $1.8$ to $3.2$, and the average number of execution steps from $24.2$ to $10.6$. In tomato harvesting, the same change results in a success rate increase from $28.0\%$ to $95.5\%$, a query count increase from $12.7$ to $22.2$, and a reduction in execution steps from $40.1$ to $19.7$. For $\tau \geq 0.7$, the success rate remains above $92\%$ in both domains. At $\tau=0.8$, the system achieves success rates of $95.5\%$ and $98.0\%$ in waste sorting and tomato harvesting, respectively. The corresponding average numbers of queries are $8.1$ and $24.3$, and the average numbers of execution steps are $10.6$ and $20.2$. Based on these results, we use $\tau=0.8$ in the remaining experiments.

We next compare four query policies at $\tau=0.8$. \textbf{All} requests feedback whenever a query can be generated. \textbf{No} performs fully autonomous execution without user feedback. \textbf{Random} requests feedback randomly. \textbf{Ours} requests feedback when the confidence of the current belief falls below the threshold and selects the query with the highest expected information gain.


Fig.~\ref{fig:sys_strategy} and Table~\ref{tab:system_eval} summarize the results. In waste sorting, \textbf{All}, \textbf{Random}, and \textbf{Ours} achieve success rates of $100.0\%$, whereas \textbf{No} achieves $80.0\%$. The average numbers of queries are $15.8$, $6.8$, and $9.2$ for \textbf{All}, \textbf{Random}, and \textbf{Ours}, respectively. In tomato harvesting, \textbf{Ours} achieves a success rate of $98.0\%$, compared to $96.5\%$ for \textbf{All}, $20.0\%$ for \textbf{No}, and $1.0\%$ for \textbf{Random}. The corresponding average numbers of queries are $20.5$, $37.3$, $0.0$, and $16.7$. The average numbers of execution steps are $20.2$, $22.4$, $36.9$, and $42.3$, respectively.

\subsubsection{Discussion}~\label{exp:sys_discussion}

The results demonstrate that human feedback can improve both task performance and execution efficiency when it is requested selectively. As the confidence threshold increases, the robot acquires additional observations through user feedback, leading to substantial improvements in task success rate. At the same time, the average execution length decreases in both domains. For example, increasing $\tau$ from $0.6$ to $0.7$ improves the success rate from $58.0\%$ to $92.5\%$ in waste sorting and from $28.0\%$ to $95.5\%$ in tomato harvesting, while reducing the average execution length by more than half in both domains.

This result suggests that informative observations can guide the planner toward more effective action sequences. If uncertainty remains unresolved, the robot may act on incorrect beliefs, resulting in unnecessary actions or failed execution attempts. By obtaining feedback at uncertain states, the planner can refine its belief earlier and select actions that are more consistent with the underlying state of the environment. Consequently, additional observations can reduce the total number of actions required to complete the task.

The comparison among query policies further highlights the importance of selecting when to request feedback. Although the \textbf{All} policy achieves high success rates, it requires substantially more user interactions. In tomato harvesting, \textbf{All} requires 37.3 queries on average, whereas \textbf{Ours} requires only 20.5 while achieving a slightly higher success rate. A similar trend is observed in waste sorting, where both methods achieve perfect success rates but \textbf{Ours} reduces the number of queries from 15.8 to 9.2. These results indicate that many feedback requests generated by continuous supervision provide limited additional information.

In contrast, the \textbf{No} policy often fails because belief errors accumulate without correction, while the \textbf{Random} policy demonstrates that simply increasing the amount of feedback is insufficient. Feedback must be acquired at states where uncertainty is likely to affect future decisions. By focusing interactions on low-confidence beliefs, the proposed approach maintains high task success, reduces unnecessary user involvement, and shortens execution length.
