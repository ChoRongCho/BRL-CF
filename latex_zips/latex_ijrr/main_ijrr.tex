\begin{abstract}
Robots operating in real environments must act from incomplete symbolic knowledge while continuously incorporating uncertain perceptual observations. This paper presents a knowledge-based belief-space planning system that treats human querying as a belief refinement operation within the robot's decision loop. The system partitions the knowledge base into domain, static, and local information; constructs an action-conditioned frontier belief over reachable symbolic successor states; updates particle weights after each observation; and computes entropy-normalized confidence before committing any state back to the knowledge base. When the belief is insufficiently confident, the robot selects the symbolic hypothesis expected to maximally reduce posterior entropy and queries the human through a feedback manager, rather than asking for action-level decisions or relying on continuous supervision. Online action selection is performed with a POMCP-style planner whose search is constrained by symbolic action applicability. We evaluate the resulting closed-loop system in waste sorting and tomato harvesting domains, including threshold analysis and policy comparisons against no-query, random-query, and always-query baselines. User-study results are reported as a secondary evaluation of interaction cost, while the main focus is the system-theoretic connection between symbolic knowledge, frontier belief update, confidence-triggered querying, and planning performance.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} belief-space planning, human-robot interaction, knowledge-based robotics, POMDP, proactive querying, planning under uncertainty
